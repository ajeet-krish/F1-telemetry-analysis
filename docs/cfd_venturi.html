CFD Venturi Tunnel

SU2 RANS simulation of the floor venturi tunnel with moving ground wall

Venturi Ground Effect Simulation

The floor of an F1 car acts as a venturi tunnel between the car body and the ground. This simulation uses SU2 CFD to solve the RANS equations for a 2D venturi profile with a moving ground wall, matching the real condition where the track surface moves relative to the car.

$$\frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_i} = 0$$
$$\bar{u}_j \frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial \bar{p}}{\partial x_i} + \nu \frac{\partial^2 \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j \partial x_j} - \frac{\partial \overline{u_i' u_j'}}{\partial x_j}$$

Simulation Setup & Mesh

The 2D venturi domain consists of:

  • Moving bottom wall: Ground plane moving at vehicle speed
  • Profiled top wall: Venturi-shaped floor with diffuser exit
  • Inlet: Uniform velocity inlet (40-80 m/s)
  • Outlet: Pressure outlet at ambient conditions
  • Symmetry: Far-field top boundary

The simulation uses the SU2 RANS solver with the SST k-omega turbulence model. The mesh is a structured quadrilateral grid with refined boundary layers (y+ < 1).

Case 2: Ride Height Sweep (h=35-80 mm)

Sweeping the ride height from 35 mm to 80 mm at fixed diffuser angle (17°) and velocity (60 m/s). As the floor gets closer to the ground, the venturi accelerates more air through the gap, increasing downforce -- but also increasing sensitivity to pitch (the porpoising mechanism).

Key physics: CL becomes more negative (more downforce) as ride height increases. This is the opposite of classical ground effect theory, which predicts stronger venturi suction at lower ride heights. The inverted trend is a Reynolds number effect: at low ride heights the boundary layers occupy a larger fraction of the gap, reducing the effective throat area and weakening the venturi pressure drop. The ratio of displacement thickness to gap height at the throat entrance (x ≈ 0.9 m) is δ*/h ≈ 0.56 at h=25 mm versus 0.14 at h=100 mm. At full-scale F1 speeds (Re > 107), boundary layers are proportionally much thinner and the classical ground effect trend is restored.

CD decreases monotonically with ride height: a larger gap reduces skin friction drag through lower surface velocities on the floor, and the weaker venturi suction reduces pressure drag. The L/D ratio improves from -5.7 at h=25 mm to -20.7 at h=100 mm, though at the cost of higher ride height sensitivity to pitch (porpoising risk).

Case 3: Diffuser Angle Sweep (α=10-20°)

The diffuser angle controls the rate of pressure recovery and the risk of flow separation. The 2026 regulations cap the diffuser angle at 17°. Sweeping from 10° to 20° at fixed ride height (50 mm) and velocity (60 m/s) shows the trade-off: steeper angles generate more downforce but risk separation.

Key physics: Downforce increases monotonically with diffuser angle, from CL = -1.71 at 10° to CL = -2.12 at 18°. The curve flattens beyond 18°, indicating the onset of diffuser separation. The expansion ratio (diffuser exit height / throat height) ranges from 1.25 at 10° to 1.51 at 20°. The 2026 regulation limit of 17° sits just below the separation threshold, capturing most of the available downforce while maintaining flow attachment margin.

Drag rises steeply with angle: CD doubles from 0.124 at 10° to 0.265 at 20°. The L/D ratio degrades from -13.8 to -8.0, showing the diminishing returns of steeper diffusers. At 20° the L/D flattens (only -8.0 vs -8.2 at 18°), consistent with separation reducing the effective expansion.

Case 4: Velocity Sweep (V=50-100 m/s)

Sweeping the freestream velocity from 50 m/s (180 km/h) to 100 m/s (360 km/h) at fixed ride height (50 mm) and diffuser angle (17°). This covers the full F1 operating range from medium-speed corners through to top speed on the straights. Downforce scales with $V^2$ in the incompressible regime, so the CL should remain nearly constant while the absolute downforce force grows quadratically.

Key physics: CL varies by less than 0.3% across the full velocity range (-1.283 at 50 m/s to -1.286 at 100 m/s). This confirms that the flow is in the incompressible regime and that the force coefficient is independent of velocity, as required by Bernoulli's principle for a fixed geometry. The absolute downforce force per unit span scales quadratically with velocity, from 513 N/m at 180 km/h to 2055 N/m at 360 km/h.

CD drops slightly from 0.201 at 50 m/s to 0.197 at 100 m/s (a 2% decrease). This is a Reynolds number effect: at higher Re the boundary layers are thinner, reducing skin friction drag. The near-constant CD validates that the mesh resolution and turbulence model capture the correct Re-independent behavior.

Force Scaling Analysis

While Cp and the non-dimensional flow fields are identical across all velocities, the dimensional forces obey the quadratic scaling law fundamental to F1 aerodynamics: downforce and drag both scale with v2. The CL coefficient is constant (0.24% variation) confirming incompressible behavior; CD decreases slightly (2.1%) as Reynolds number increases, reducing skin friction. This yields a small L/D improvement at higher speeds.

Code

from src.cfd.su2_runner import SU2Config, SU2Solver, MeshGenerator

# Generate structured quad mesh
mesh = MeshGenerator.venturi_2d(
    ride_height=0.05,
    diffuser_angle=17.0,
    n_x=200, n_y=80,
)

# Configure SU2 RANS solver
config = SU2Config(
    reynolds_number=203_000,
    reynolds_length=0.05,
    solver="INC_RANS",
    turbulence_model="SST",
    inc_velocity_init=(60.0, 0.0, 0.0),
    cfl_number=0.5,
    iterations=2000,
    marker_walls=("ground", "floor"),
    marker_inlets=("inlet",),
    marker_outlets=("outlet",),
    marker_moving=("ground",),
    moving_wall=True,
    inc_inlet_type="PRESSURE_INLET",
)
config.write("venturi.cfg")

# Run solver
solver = SU2Solver()
result = solver.run("venturi.cfg", "venturi.su2")
print(f"CL = {result.cl:.2f}, CD = {result.cd:.4f}")
